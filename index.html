Drone Circle

The slowness exists between what you can imagine and what you can make. The idea of reducing this distance may seem especially topical at the moment, but it has long been the core pursuit of creative technology.

1950s Cologne, Karlheinz Stockhausen drew shapes on paper to score Studie II. one of the earliest pieces of electronic music. Drawing rectangles for notation and triangles for amplitude, these sketches predate both the MIDI roll and the Envelope.

1960s England, Daphne Oram invented Oramics, a machine that reads ink sketches on film strips to describe specific sound parameters: pitch, timbre, volume, vibrato, duration. The photocells then read the drawings and outputs the sound in real time.

In a typical modular synth session, knobs, sliders, cables, buttons all need to be physically operated. Of course, this labour is preceded by some kind of intention. This intention can be specific or vague, with high or low predictability in mind. The unclear intentions and incorrect assumptions leave room for happy accidents—some of the best moments.

At 56, our commercial work begins with setting predictable goals and clarifying intentions. However, due to the nature of business, the creative process is often shrouded in chaos. Lately we've been building agentic workflows to make this chaos more accessible. With this framing, while playing around with modular synths, we wondered what it would be like if we replaced the physical labour with natural language.
